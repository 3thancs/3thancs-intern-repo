**Why are PRs important in a team workflow?**
* To ensure that team members can **spot bugs (logic or security vulnerabilities)** that I may miss out. 
* To **acquire senior mentorship feedback** and apply it to projects, where they could teach me on how I could write modular code which improves workflow productivity.
* To **trace the current bugs** back to the documented PR approval, discussion and comparisons of tasks, which links to the business requirements and the code.
* A **gatekeeper** to ensure modular tests should be run after opening the PR. It should lock the merge branch button until further approval.

**What makes a well-structured PR?**
* It consists of five components: 
  * **The Description**: The brief overview of the changes that occurred and the explanation of the change
  * **Visual Evidence**: A screenshot evidence of 'before' and 'after'
  *  **Scope Control**: A small code block is ideal, since the reviewer is focused on one specific task instead of being overwhelmed by large code blocks
  * **Technical Implementation**: Team members should justify the architectural choice
  * **Testing Workflow**: Allow reviewers to understand the workflow process of a team member's work

**What did you learn from reviewing an open-source PR?**
  * To digest feedback provided by reviewers (team members or mentors). It welcomes **constructive feedback instead of personal attacks**, where instead of mentioning 'The logic is incorrect' to 'It would be great to further amend this specific logic'.

* It teaches me how to **plan projects with structure critically**. For example, I would review issues frequently missed by team members - such as obsolete or incompatible language versions and hardware limitations. 

* It taught me to **trace back logic** by my team members. This allowed me to understand their logical reasoning by understanding the impact of the system given the existing codebase. In short, this is how I could become a superior writer by being a constructive reader.
